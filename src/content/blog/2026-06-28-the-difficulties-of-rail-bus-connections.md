The recently released [Victorian Auditor-General's Office inquiry](https://www.audit.vic.gov.au/report/improving-bus-services/?section=) into the [Department of Transport's Bus Plan](https://www.vic.gov.au/sites/default/files/2023-09/victorias-bus-plan-bus-reform-roadmap.pdf) placed a heavy emphasis on timetabling 'High priority connections'

![image.png](/image.png)

When trains and buses are both frequent, connections matter less and less. 

Special timetabling that allows for these connections should be seen as a bonus, not something that commuters should rely on. It obviously makes sense for Night Buses to wait and be scheduled around rail connections because of how infrequent they are. 

Let's take a look at Glen Waverley Station which has one high-frequency route (902), nine lower-frequency routes, and a Night Bus. Between 7-8:30am, a train heads towards the city every 7 minutes on average. How many of these routes should be timetabled to connect?

- 902: 76km route passes through nearly a dozen railway stations, it can't be timetabled to wait without dwell time. 
- 736: Operates in both directions at a
- 742: 3 railway stations, operates in both directions
- 734
- 737: 3 railway stations, passengers travelling towards Monash should change for the 703 at Syndal.
- 753
- 754

For a train line operating every 5 minutes or better during peak, according to this definition, ++every bus will connect.++ Let's compare this to the average wait time during AM peak towards the city on the most popular lines: 

- Dandenong Group: 4 minutes
- Frankston Line: 5 minutes
- Glen Waverley: 7 minutes
- Ringwood Group: 5 minutes (inc. express)

Clearly bus-train connections are a greater issue for inter-peak and off-peak services.
